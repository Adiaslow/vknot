Adam Murray / June 14, 2026

1. Introduction

1.1 Imaging Artifacts in Biological Photography

TODO — Motivates from the practical problem: routine biological photography of petri dishes (colony counting, antimicrobial assays, etc.) using consumer-grade cameras under typical laboratory lighting frequently produces images with bright reflective regions that do not correspond to biological features. These artifacts confound downstream image analysis pipelines (e.g., colony segmentation in FIJI). Frames the problem as deserving a principled treatment.

1.2 The Cross-Domain Geometry of the Specimen Surface

TODO — Surfaces the observation that the same geometric optics governs cranial-window imaging in two-photon microscopy, slide-based pathology, microfluidic device photography, and petri dish imaging. The recurring structure — a curved liquid interface above an opaque or semi-transparent specimen, viewed through air or an additional transparent layer — appears across imaging modalities. The framework developed here applies to all of them.

2. The Layered Optical System

2.1 Geometry of the Petri Dish, Agar, and Optional Liquid Layer

TODO — Establishes the cross-sectional geometry. Standard 100 mm × 15 mm polystyrene petri dish with 18 mL of MRS agar (depth ≈ 2.3 mm). Optional aqueous liquid layer on top of the agar (e.g., poured bacterial suspension). Optional lid with small air gap. Defines coordinate system and notation. Justifies the rotationally-symmetric cross-section as fully capturing the relevant geometry.

2.2 Meniscus Formation and the Young-Laplace Equation

TODO — Derives the meniscus profile from the Young-Laplace equation balancing surface tension against gravity. Introduces the capillary length λ_c = √(γ/ρg) and shows that for water, λ_c ≈ 2.7 mm. Presents the exact wall-meniscus solution and justifies the cosh-based approximation used in the simulator. Treats both the agar surface (set in place when the agar solidified) and any liquid layer poured on top.

2.3 Material Properties at the Wavelengths of Interest

TODO — Tabulates the refractive indices and absorption coefficients of each material in the system across the visible spectrum: air (n=1), polystyrene dish/lid (n≈1.59), agar gel (n≈1.33, mildly absorbing in the blue producing the amber color), aqueous mixtures (n≈1.33, nearly transparent). Notes the partially-specular nature of the agar surface (the gel is slightly opalescent) versus the strongly-specular nature of clear liquid interfaces. These material parameters are what the simulator uses.

3. Reflection at Smooth and Curved Surfaces

3.1 Specular and Diffuse Reflection: A Material Property Distinction

TODO — Defines specular reflection (directional, governed by the law of reflection) and diffuse reflection (Lambertian, scattered uniformly across the hemisphere). Frames real materials as a continuum, with most surfaces exhibiting some mix of both. Argues that the specular component is what generates imaging artifacts because it carries spatial information from sources that have nothing to do with the specimen.

3.2 The Law of Reflection

TODO — States the law (angle of incidence equals angle of reflection, both measured from the surface normal). Brief derivation from Fermat’s principle for completeness. Sets up notation: incoming direction d_in, surface normal n̂, reflected direction d_out = d_in − 2(d_in · n̂)n̂.

3.3 The Curved-Surface Problem: Continuous Families of Mirror Orientations

TODO — The key conceptual move of the article. A curved surface is, locally, a family of flat mirrors at varying orientations. For any pair (light source, camera), there exists a continuous set of surface points whose local normals bisect the source-to-point and point-to-camera directions. This is the geometric reason that any non-zero meniscus magnitude guarantees specular paths between any overhead-ish light source and a directly-overhead camera. Quantifies this for the cosh meniscus model.

Remark

TODO — Picture-level version: when a surface is curved, every angle is available somewhere on it. If light comes from many directions, some point on the curve will always be oriented to bounce it into the camera. The fix is to make either the light or the surface uniform in direction — directional lighting from one place, or a flat surface with one consistent normal.

4.2 Total Internal Reflection

TODO — Derives the critical angle from Snell’s law for the limit case n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ × 1. Shows that for the air-liquid interface, the critical angle from below is around 49°. Notes that this matters for light trapped between the lid and the liquid surface, where multiple bounces can occur before light escapes.

4.3 The Fresnel Equations: Energy Partition at an Interface

TODO — States the Fresnel equations for s-polarized and p-polarized light, including Brewster’s angle where the p-component goes to zero. Discusses the averaging convention for unpolarized light. Plots reflection coefficient vs. incidence angle for the air-liquid interface to make the angle dependence visible. This is what the simulator uses to compute how much energy reflects vs. transmits at each interaction.

4.4 Beer-Lambert Attenuation Through Absorbing Media

TODO — States the Beer-Lambert law I(d) = I₀ exp(−αd) for intensity decay through an absorbing medium of absorption coefficient α and path length d. Tabulates approximate α values for MRS agar at R, G, B wavelengths. Notes that this is what gives the agar its amber color: blue is preferentially absorbed, leaving the transmitted light yellow-amber.

5. Imaging Configurations

5.1 The Overhead-Lighting Failure Mode

TODO — Applies the curved-surface argument of §3.3 to a typical room-lighting configuration. Quantitative analysis: shows that for any non-zero meniscus magnitude, there exists a continuous set of surface points contributing specular paths to a directly-overhead camera. Recommends turning the simulator to “overhead lighting only, liquid layer on” to see the result.

5.2 Directional Off-Axis Illumination

TODO — The geometric solution. For a single point source at angle θ above the horizon, on the same side as the camera (camera directly above), the surface normal required to specularly bisect the path is at angle (θ+φ)/2 from vertical. By choosing θ appropriately, this required normal is outside the range that any point on the near-horizontal agar/liquid surface presents. Practical recommendations: lamp at 30°–45° above the table, with the room lights off.

5.3 Flattening the Surface: Cover Slips and Spread Plates

TODO — The Peichao reframe. Rather than redirecting the light, flatten the surface so its normals are all parallel. Two implementations: an optical cover slip (used in cranial-window imaging, slide microscopy) and a methodological one (the spread plate technique, where bacterial suspension is absorbed into the agar rather than left as a curved liquid layer on top). Both collapse the family of normal orientations to a single direction.

5.4 Polarization-Based Specular Suppression

TODO — Brewster’s angle and partial polarization of specular reflections from dielectric surfaces. A linear polarizer oriented orthogonally to the reflected light suppresses specular highlights while leaving diffuse scattering largely intact. Practical implementation with phone-camera polarizing filters.

5.5 Imaging from Below

TODO — Inversion of the imaging geometry. The dish bottom is the flattest available surface; backlit imaging through it bypasses most of the curved-interface problems entirely. This is the technique used by clinical colony counters and many automated imaging systems.

6.2 Limitations of the Geometric Optics Framework

TODO — What this treatment ignores: diffraction effects at features comparable to or smaller than the wavelength of light (relevant for high-resolution microscopy but not for macroscopic petri-dish imaging); Mie scattering by particulate matter (relevant for densely-cultured plates); birefringence and polarization rotation by structured biological materials; quantum-mechanical phenomena (irrelevant at this scale). Notes that for the macroscopic imaging problem treated here, the geometric optics framework is adequate.
