## 3. Reflection at Smooth and Curved Surfaces

### 3.1 Specular and Diffuse Reflection: A Material Property Distinction

_TODO — Defines specular reflection (directional, governed by the law of reflection) and diffuse reflection (Lambertian, scattered uniformly across the hemisphere). Argues that the specular component is what generates imaging artifacts because it carries spatial information from sources that have nothing to do with the specimen._

### 3.2 The Law of Reflection

_TODO — States the law (angle of incidence equals angle of reflection, both measured from the surface normal). Brief derivation from Fermat's principle for completeness. Sets up notation: incoming direction d_in, surface normal n̂, reflected direction d_out = d_in − 2(d_in · n̂)n̂._

### 3.3 The Curved-Surface Problem: Continuous Families of Mirror Orientations

_TODO — The key conceptual move of the article. A curved surface is, locally, a family of flat mirrors at varying orientations. For any pair (light source, camera), there exists a continuous set of surface points whose local normals bisect the source-to-point and point-to-camera directions. This is the geometric reason that any non-zero meniscus magnitude guarantees specular paths between any overhead-ish light source and a directly-overhead camera. Quantifies this for the cosh meniscus model._

<Remark>
_TODO — Picture-level version: when a surface is curved, every angle is available somewhere on it. If light comes from many directions, some point on the curve will always be oriented to bounce it into the camera. The fix is to make either the light or the surface uniform in direction — directional lighting from one place, or a flat surface with one consistent normal._
</Remark>

### 5.2 Directional Off-Axis Illumination

_TODO — The geometric solution. For a single point source at angle θ above the horizon, on the same side as the camera (camera directly above), the surface normal required to specularly bisect the path is at angle (θ+φ)/2 from vertical. By choosing θ appropriately, this required normal is outside the range that any point on the near-horizontal agar/liquid surface presents. Practical recommendations: lamp at 30°–45° above the table, with the room lights off._

### 5.3 Flattening the Surface: Cover Slips and Spread Plates

_TODO — The Peichao reframe. Rather than redirecting the light, flatten the surface so its normals are all parallel. Two implementations: an optical cover slip (used in cranial-window imaging, slide microscopy) and a methodological one (the spread plate technique, where bacterial suspension is absorbed into the agar rather than left as a curved liquid layer on top). Both collapse the family of normal orientations to a single direction._
